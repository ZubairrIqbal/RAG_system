CP77-400 Sugarcane Variety
Origin and Development
CP77-400 is a sugarcane variety developed by the Louisiana Agricultural Experiment Station in the United States. This variety was released in the late 1970s and has been widely cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions.
Morphological Characteristics

Plant height: 2.5 to 3.0 meters
Stalk diameter: Medium to thick (25-30mm)
Stalk color: Green with purple tinge
Growth habit: Erect and vigorous
Leaf characteristics: Long, narrow leaves with good canopy coverage
Bud prominence: Moderately prominent

Sugar Content and Quality

Sucrose content: 14-16%
Commercial Cane Sugar (CCS): 12-14%
Juice purity: 85-88%
Juice extraction: 65-70%
Sugar recovery: High quality with excellent crystallization properties

Yield Performance

Cane yield: 80-100 tons per hectare
Sugar yield: 10-14 tons per hectare
Plant crop yield: 90-110 tons/ha
Ratoon crop yield: 75-95 tons/ha
Number of millable canes: 80,000-100,000 per hectare

Maturity and Harvesting

Maturity period: Medium (10-12 months)
Optimal harvest time: December to March
Early season sugar: Moderate
Mid-season sugar: Good
Late season sugar: Excellent
Ratooning ability: Good (3-4 productive ratoons)

Disease Resistance Profile
Resistant to:

Mosaic virus disease
Rust (Puccinia melanocephala)
Leaf scald

Moderately resistant to:

Sugarcane borer
Red rot (Colletotrichum falcatum)

Susceptible to:

Smut (Ustilago scitaminea)
Red rot in certain conditions
Grassy shoot disease

Pest Resistance

Early shoot borer: Moderate resistance
Top borer: Susceptible
White grubs: Moderate tolerance
Termites: Requires protection

Type: Well-drained loamy soils preferred

Water Management

Irrigation requirement: Medium
Critical irrigation stages: Tillering, grand growth, and formative phase
Water stress tolerance: Moderate
Drought tolerance: Moderate
Flood tolerance: Low

Nutrient Management
NPK Requirements:

Nitrogen: 150-200 kg/ha
Phosphorus: 60-80 kg/ha
Potassium: 100-150 kg/ha

Application schedule:

Basal: 50% N, 100% P, 50% K at planting
First top dressing: 25% N at 30-45 days
Second top dressing: 25% N, 50% K at 90-120 days

Micronutrients:

Zinc: 25 kg ZnSO4/ha
Iron: Foliar spray if deficiency
Manganese: 10-15 kg/ha if needed

Cultivation Practices
Planting:

Season: February-March (Spring), July-August (Autumn)
Seed rate: 30,000-40,000 three-budded setts per hectare
Row spacing: 90-120 cm
Seed treatment: Fungicide and insecticide treatment recommended

Intercultural operations:

Gap filling: Within 30 days of planting
Earthing up: 90-120 days after planting
Weed control: 2-3 manual weedings or herbicides
Trash mulching: Beneficial for moisture conservation

Advantages

High sugar recovery rate suitable for commercial processing
Good commercial cane sugar percentage
Excellent juice quality for sugar manufacturing
Suitable for mechanized harvesting due to erect growth
Good ratoon crop performance
Adaptable to various soil types
Moderate pest resistance reduces protection costs
Good standing ability, low lodging

Limitations

Susceptible to smut disease requires seed treatment
Moderate red rot susceptibility in high humidity
Requires timely pest management
Not suitable for water-logged conditions
Lower drought tolerance compared to newer varieties
Requires adequate fertilizer application for optimal yield
Frost sensitive, not for extreme cold regions

Economic Considerations

Cost of cultivation: Medium
Input requirements: Moderate to high
Market acceptance: Excellent
Processing quality: Superior
Return on investment: Good in suitable conditions
Risk factors: Disease management critical

Recommended Growing Regions

Tropical plains with assured irrigation
Subtropical regions with adequate rainfall
Areas with 10-12 month growing season
Regions with mechanized harvesting facilities
Well-drained agricultural lands
Areas with good sugar factory proximity

Best Management Practices

Use disease-free, healthy seed material
Proper seed treatment before planting
Maintain optimal plant population
Follow recommended fertilizer schedule
Ensure timely irrigation during critical stages
Regular monitoring for pests and diseases
Proper weed management in early stages
Harvest at optimal maturity for maximum sugar
